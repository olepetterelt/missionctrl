Eclipse facts
Total solar eclipses are only possible because the Moon and Sun appear almost exactly the same size in our sky. The Moon is 400× smaller than the Sun, but also 400× closer — a cosmic coincidence unique in the solar system.
Saros cycle: Eclipses repeat in an 18-year, 11-day, 8-hour cycle called the Saros. Every eclipse is related to another one that occurred 18 years earlier and predicts one 18 years in the future.
The Moon is slowly moving away from Earth at ~3.8 cm/year. In ~600 million years, the Moon will appear too small to completely cover the Sun, and total solar eclipses will no longer be possible.
Blood Moons occur during total lunar eclipses because Earth's atmosphere bends and filters sunlight, casting a red glow on the Moon. The colour depends on how much dust and clouds are in Earth's atmosphere.
Annular eclipses occur when the Moon is near apogee (furthest from Earth) and appears smaller than the Sun, leaving a ring of sunlight — the "ring of fire" — visible around the Moon.
Diamond ring effect: In the last seconds before totality, a single bead of sunlight shines through a lunar valley, creating a dazzling diamond ring appearance.
