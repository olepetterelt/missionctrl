Integrated Flight Tests
Flight 1 — IFT-1
Apr 20, 2023 · Boca Chica, TX
Partial success
First integrated launch attempt
Both stages ignited and the vehicle cleared the pad. Multiple Raptor engines failed during ascent. Vehicle began tumbling ~4 min after launch. Flight termination system activated. Pad infrastructure severely damaged (no flame deflector).
Ignition Liftoff Max-Q survival Stage separation Controlled flight Landing burn
Flight 2 — IFT-2
Nov 18, 2023 · Boca Chica, TX
Partial success
Stage separation achieved for first time
Hot staging separation worked — a major milestone. Super Heavy executed a flip and boostback burn before FTS was triggered. Ship reached space (~148 km) before losing control during entry. Both vehicles lost.
Liftoff Max-Q survival Stage separation (hot staging) Ship reaches space Booster controlled return Ship reentry
Flight 3 — IFT-3
Mar 14, 2024 · Boca Chica, TX
Partial success
Payload door open/close · Propellant transfer demo
Longest flight to date. Ship completed most of its planned trajectory around Earth. Payload bay door opened and closed in space. In-space propellant transfer demonstrated. Both vehicles lost during return phases — ship lost contact during reentry.
Full trajectory flight Payload door operation Propellant transfer Booster flip maneuver Booster soft splashdown Ship controlled reentry
Flight 4 — IFT-4
Jun 6, 2024 · Boca Chica, TX
Major success
Booster splashdown ✓ · Ship controlled reentry ✓
Breakthrough flight. Super Heavy booster successfully executed a controlled splashdown in the Gulf of Mexico. Ship survived reentry with significant tile damage and completed a controlled splashdown in the Indian Ocean. Both vehicles survived.
Booster controlled splashdown Ship reentry survival Ship splashdown Tile performance data
Flight 5 — IFT-5
Oct 13, 2024 · Boca Chica, TX
Historic success
Booster caught by mechazilla arms ✓ · Ship splashdown ✓
One of the most impressive rocket maneuvers in history. Super Heavy returned to the launch site and was caught by the Mechazilla tower arms — the first time a booster of this size was caught mid-air. Ship completed another successful splashdown.
Booster catch (world first) Precision tower return Ship reentry + splashdown Rapid reusability demonstration

Nov 19, 2024 · Boca Chica, TX
Success

Second successful booster catch. Ship catch was attempted but the catch was not executed due to conditions — ship performed a controlled splashdown. Further propulsion and tile improvements demonstrated.

Why Starship matters
Starship is designed to be fully and rapidly reusable — both the booster and the ship. If achieved, it would reduce the cost of getting to orbit by 10-100× compared to current rockets.

It is NASA's only selected lunar lander for Artemis (HLS) and is SpaceX's intended vehicle for crewed Mars missions. The ability to refuel in orbit (enabled by orbital propellant transfer) allows it to carry much heavier payloads beyond Earth orbit than any rocket in history.

Elon Musk has stated a target cost of under $10M per launch at scale — compared to ~$4B for SLS and ~$70M for Falcon 9.
